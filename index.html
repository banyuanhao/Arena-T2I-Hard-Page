Existing faithfulness benchmarks rely on synthetic templates, LLM rewriting, or short curated captions. Even the longest-form predecessor (DPG-Bench) averages only ~70 words per prompt. Arena-T2I Hard draws from real T2I-arena user votes and selects for compositional difficulty: ~430 words and ~30 decomposed yes/no questions per prompt.

Abstract

Existing T2I faithfulness benchmarks rely on simple atomic instructions on which top systems already score near-perfectly, so a single binary VLM-judge score no longer reveals which constraints a model fails. We introduce Arena-T2I Hard, a 310-prompt stress benchmark drawn from real arena T2I logs, with ~30 decomposed yes/no constraints per prompt across six categories. The strongest system we evaluate reaches only 0.855, with a 33 pp spread across 11 systems—and high public-arena rankings fail to predict faithfulness, confirming that holistic preference scores prioritize aesthetics over fine-grained prompt adherence.

The same dependency-aware checklist that scores the benchmark also works as a post-training reward: combined with a Bradley-Terry aesthetic reward via group-decoupled normalization (GDPO), it improves both faithfulness and aesthetics on SD3.5-Medium and FLUX.1-dev, beating every single-reward, naive weighted-sum, and 4-reward ensemble baseline.

The Benchmark

Arena-T2I Hard

All prompts are sampled from a public text-to-image arena leaderboard (Jan–Mar 2026), so the distribution reflects what real users actually ask of T2I systems. After NSFW/PII/invalid-prompt filtering and manual review, we select 310 deliberately compositional prompts—long, multi-entity, with attributes, spatial relations, counts, and stylistic constraints stacked together—balanced across six visual-style categories.

How We Score: Dependency-Aware Checklist

A frozen LLM decomposer maps each prompt to a DAG of yes/no questions; a VLM judge answers them in BFS order. If a parent fails, its descendants are scored NO without a further VLM call—avoiding the inflated scores flat checklists produce when an attribute fires on the wrong object. The faithfulness score is the yes-ratio over the questions.

“A red cat sitting on a blue chair in a garden”
q0  Is there a cat? (root)  → YES
q1  Is the cat red? (depends_on: q0)  → YES
q2  Is there a chair? (root)  → NO
q3  Is the chair blue? (depends_on: q2)  → auto-NO (parent failed, no VLM call)
q4  Is the cat sitting on the chair? (depends_on: q0, q2)  → auto-NO
q5  Is there a garden? (root)  → YES

Illustrative decomposition. Because the chair is absent (q2 = NO), its attribute (q3) and the relation that requires it (q4) are zeroed without querying the judge. The faithfulness score is the yes-ratio over the faithfulness questions.

Bonus

The Checklist as a Post-Training Reward

Single rewards don't transfer across axes: BT preference rewards lift aesthetics but push faithfulness down, and vice-versa. We combine the checklist (faithfulness) with a BT reward (aesthetics) under group-decoupled normalization (GDPO), which standardizes each reward within its rollout group before combining. On FLUX.1-dev this lifts both signals (+10.5% faithfulness, +3.5% PickScore), and under the MMRB2 pairwise protocol our Faith + Pick run is the strongest row on both SD3.5-Medium and FLUX.1-dev—beating every single-reward, ensemble, and iterative-SFT baseline (a 1,899-vote human study agrees, 64.1% overall).
